Jackie Robinson

I admire Jackie Robinson because of his passion and perseverance. At the time of him playing professional baseball, the sport had been segregated and put into two different leagues based on race. When switching from the Negro League to the Major Baseball League, he knew the risks that were going to come with it. He knew that there were people out there that didn’t like him and weren’t going to agree with him playing simply because of the color of his skin. He also knew that he couldn’t retaliate against them, because then the spotlight would be put on him, but not in a positive manner. With all these roadblocks in front of him, he still played, and played his best, and because of this he officially broke the color barrier in the sport of baseball. On top of all of that, he was also an advocate for civil rights and was a member of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP).

Jack Roosevelt Robinson, also know as Jackie Robinson is an African American baseball player known for breaking the color barrier in baseball. He was born on January 31, 1919 in Georgia, but grew up in California. Growing up, he was the only black family on the block. Sports was something that Robinson always excelled at. While attending school, he played 4 sports: football, basketball, baseball, and track. In 1941, he became UCLA’s first student to receive a varsity letter in four sports. After leaving University, he went to the Army where he became second lieutenant. As these were segregated times, he got arrested for not giving up his seat on a bus. Because of this, he acquired an honorable discharge.

After being discharged from the Army, Robsinson went ahead and started to play professional baseball in the Negro Leagues. Baseball at this time was segregated, and had two different leagues. Soon after, the president of the Brooklyn Dodgers, Branch Rickey, contacted Robinson and wanted him to play for the Major League Baseball (MLB). In 1946 Robinson joined a farm team for the Brooklyn dodgers. While playing with them, he acquired a .349 batting average and a .985 fielding percentage, and with these stats he went on to play with the Brooklyn Dodgers on April 15, 1947. This was huge, because he broke the color barrier and became the first African American to play in the MLB. Despite all the racism and harassment experienced, he still broke barriers and made history.
